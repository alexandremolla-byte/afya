## POST 1 — Founder story (publish on launch day)

I built AFYA because of my uncle.

He was diagnosed with type 2 diabetes at 54. The doctor gave him a list of medications, told him to "watch his diet", and sent him home.

No one explained what a normal blood sugar actually looks like. No one told him that the eba he'd eaten every day for 40 years was spiking his glucose. No one followed up between the quarterly clinic visits.

He managed — barely — mostly through trial and error and WhatsApp messages to distant relatives who happened to be nurses.

11 million Nigerians are living with diabetes right now. Over half don't know they have it. The ones who do know are often managing alone, without the kind of daily guidance that actually changes outcomes.

So we built AFYA.

It's an AI health companion — specifically designed for Nigerians managing diabetes, hypertension, pregnancy, and general wellness. It gives you the kind of daily support that a knowledgeable health-savvy friend would: medication reminders, vitals tracking, and answers to the health questions that feel too small to call your doctor about but too important to ignore.

It's free to start. It works on any smartphone. No download needed.

We're just getting started — and I'd love to hear from anyone working on health access in Africa.

## POST 2 — Shocking statistic + problem framing

1 in 3 Nigerian adults has high blood pressure.

Most of them don't know.

Not because they haven't had the chance to check — but because hypertension has no symptoms. You can walk around for years with a blood pressure of 160/100, feeling completely fine, while it quietly damages your heart, kidneys, and brain.

In Nigeria, we discover hypertension the hard way: during a stroke. During a heart attack. During a hospital admission for something else entirely.

The fix isn't complicated. A ₦3,000 blood pressure monitor at any pharmacy. A 5-minute check. A doctor's appointment if the numbers are wrong.

The gap isn't medical — it's informational and habitual. People don't check because no one has made it a habit, no one has explained what the numbers mean, and there's no system nudging them to do it consistently.

That's what we're trying to change with AFYA.

If you know someone managing hypertension in Nigeria — share this with them. Knowledge and consistency are the two things that protect you from complications.

## POST 4 — Education post (women's health angle)

The most common symptom of diabetes in Nigerian women isn't thirst or frequent urination.

It's recurrent yeast infections.

High blood sugar creates exactly the environment yeast thrives in. Women who have 3, 4, 5 yeast infections a year — treated each time with antifungals, never investigated further — are often living with undiagnosed diabetes.

Other symptoms that get missed:

→ Dark patches on the neck and armpits (acanthosis nigricans) — commonly seen in Nigerian women, often dismissed as a skin issue or blamed on deodorant
→ Fatigue attributed to stress, motherhood, or "just life"
→ PCOS — strongly linked to insulin resistance, often not connected to diabetes risk in Nigerian clinics

Over 60% of Nigerian women with type 2 diabetes were undiagnosed at the time of a major complication.

If you've had repeated yeast infections, unexplained fatigue, or dark patches on your skin — ask your doctor for a fasting blood sugar test. It's a simple blood draw. It costs less than ₦2,000 at most labs.

Early detection changes everything.

## POST 5 — Pricing and accessibility angle

A lot of "AI health" tools are built for people who already have access to good healthcare.

They assume you have a primary care physician. That you can book a follow-up in 48 hours. That you're already tracking your health in a structured way.

Most Nigerians don't have that.

The average Nigerian with type 2 diabetes sees a doctor once every 3 months — if they can afford it and if the clinic isn't overwhelmed. Between those visits, they're on their own.

That's the gap AFYA sits in.

We charge ₦2,500 per month for Premium — roughly the price of a plate of rice at a sit-down restaurant. Free tier covers the core features for anyone who can't afford that.

We made a deliberate choice not to price in dollars, not to price for the diaspora, not to build for the Nigerian who has options. We built for the person who doesn't.

## POST 6 — Behind the name

Why did we call it AFYA?

AFYA (pronounced ah-FYA) is the Swahili word for health. Swahili is spoken across East and Central Africa — but the word felt right for what we're building: a health companion for the continent, starting in Nigeria.

We almost called it something else. Something that sounded more like a SaaS product. Something with a .io domain and a wordmark in Helvetica.

Then we thought about who we were actually building for. A 58-year-old woman in Enugu managing hypertension. A father in Lagos who's just been diagnosed with type 2 diabetes and doesn't know what he's allowed to eat. A pregnant woman in Abuja worried about her blood pressure.

AFYA felt like something you'd say to someone you cared about.

"How is your AFYA?" — how is your health, how are you doing, are you taking care of yourself?

That's the app we wanted to build.

## POST 7 — Social proof / testimonial style

"I've been taking Metformin for 2 years. Nobody ever explained why my stomach hurt every morning until I asked AFYA."

That was a message from a user last week.

The answer: Metformin should be taken with food, not on an empty stomach. Most patients are never told this. They just endure the nausea, assume it's permanent, and eventually stop taking their medication.

Medication non-adherence is one of the biggest killers in diabetes management in Nigeria. Not the disease itself — the gap between the prescription and the daily habit.

A lot of what AFYA does isn't glamorous AI. It's answering the questions people are too embarrassed to ask their doctor, too busy to Google properly, or can't afford to book an appointment to ask:

"Can I eat eba if I have diabetes?"
"My blood pressure reading was 145/92 — should I be worried?"
"I missed two days of my medication — what do I do?"

These questions have good answers. Getting them shouldn't require a clinic appointment.

That's why we built this.

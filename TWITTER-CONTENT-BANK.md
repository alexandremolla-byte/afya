### THREAD: Understanding Blood Sugar Ranges in Nigeria (8 tweets)

**1/8 - HOOK**
Your doctor said your blood glucose is "high" but you have no idea what that means in real numbers. 125? 200? 95? This thread breaks down what normal actually looks like in Nigeria. Save this. (1/8)

**2/8**
Normal fasting blood sugar (before you eat anything): Below 100 mg/dL or below 5.6 mmol/L.

That's your goal. If your last fasting test was 110 or higher, you're in the prediabetic range. This is the critical window. (2/8)

**3/8**
Two hours after eating (post-meal glucose):
- Normal: Below 140 mg/dL (7.8 mmol/L)
- Prediabetes: 140–199 mg/dL
- Diabetes: 200 or higher

This is why your doctor asks "how long since you ate?" (3/8)

**4/8**
Then there's HbA1c — the 3-month average. Think of it as your blood sugar's report card.
- Normal: Below 5.7%
- Prediabetes: 5.7–6.4%
- Diabetes: 6.5% or above

One high test doesn't confirm it. Your doctor wants 2 tests. (4/8)

**5/8**
Pro tip for Nigeria: Most labs use mg/dL. Some teaching hospitals use mmol/L. To convert mmol/L to mg/dL, multiply by 18. So 6.0 mmol/L = 108 mg/dL. Keep this ratio in your notes app. (5/8)

**6/8**
Here's the hard truth: You can feel 100% fine at 150 mg/dL. Diabetes doesn't announce itself with symptoms early. This is why it's called "the silent killer." You only know because you checked. (6/8)

**7/8**
If your numbers are creeping up, you have options:
- Walk 30 mins after eating (drops glucose naturally)
- Swap white rice for ofada rice
- Cut sugary drinks (Malt, Fanta, energy drinks hit hard)
- Test weekly until it stabilizes

Action beats medication at this stage. (7/8)

### THREAD: Normal vs. High Blood Pressure in Nigeria (10 tweets)

**1/10 - HOOK**
"140 over 90" — what does that even mean? And is YOUR number high? This thread explains BP like you're not a doctor. Share this with anyone over 40 in your family. (1/10)

**2/10**
Two numbers. Two stories.
- TOP number (systolic): Pressure when your heart beats
- BOTTOM number (diastolic): Pressure when your heart rests

Both matter. But if even ONE is high, you have hypertension. Both numbers are in the definition. (2/10)

**3/10**
Normal blood pressure: Below 120/80 mmHg. That's the goal. If you're here, you're winning. Keep doing what you're doing.

**Elevated:** 120–129 AND below 80. Warning sign. This is the time to act. (3/10)

**4/10**
Stage 1 Hypertension: 130–139/80–89
This is where many Nigerians are — and many don't know it.
Your organs aren't screaming yet. But damage is starting. Lifestyle changes work here.

Stage 2: 140/90 and above. You likely need medication. (4/10)

**5/10**
Hypertensive Crisis (EMERGENCY): 180/120 or higher
Headache, shortness of breath, chest pain, nosebleed? Get to a hospital NOW. This is organ damage happening in real time. Don't wait. (5/10)

**6/10**
Why is it so common in Nigeria?
1. Genetics — West African populations have higher rates than most
2. Salt intake — it's in Maggi, crayfish, smoked foods, even bread
3. Stress (Lagos traffic, unpaid salaries, power cuts, cost of living)
4. Low activity, high processed foods (6/10)

**7/10**
The scariest part: You can have stage 2 hypertension and feel ZERO symptoms. No headache. No dizziness. No chest pain. Your kidney is quietly losing function. Your heart is slowly thickening. You feel fine. That's the trap. (7/10)

**8/10**
"Can it be cured?"
Primary hypertension (the most common kind) can't be permanently cured. But it can be completely controlled. Some people drop it through weight loss and lifestyle alone. Most need lifelong medication. Both are fine. (8/10)

**9/10**
Never stop medication because you "feel better" or because your reading came down. The medicine IS why you feel better. Stopping it causes immediate spike. Your body doesn't return to normal — it returns to hypertensive. The treatment is why you're well. (9/10)

### THREAD: Metformin Side Effects & How to Handle Them (11 tweets)

**1/11 - HOOK**
You're on Metformin for diabetes and the nausea/diarrhea has you thinking about quitting. DON'T. This thread explains what's normal, what's not, and how to tough it out (or switch if you really can't). (1/11)

**2/11**
Metformin is the cheapest, most effective first-line diabetes drug in Nigeria. Generic versions cost ₦1,500–3,000/month. Effectiveness is proven. But the side effects scare people off in week 1. Don't quit early. (2/11)

**3/11**
Most common Metformin side effects:
- Nausea (mild to moderate)
- Diarrhea or loose stools
- Stomach discomfort
- Metallic taste
- Loss of appetite

These are TEMPORARY. Your body adapts. Usually 2–4 weeks. Some people adjust in days. (3/11)

**4/11**
Why does this happen?
Metformin works on your gut bacteria. It changes your microbiome balance to improve insulin sensitivity. Your gut is literally being reprogrammed. The discomfort is a sign it's working. Yes, really. (4/11)

**5/11**
To minimize side effects:
1. Take it WITH FOOD (this is crucial — don't take it on empty stomach)
2. Start with 500mg at night with dinner (gives you time to sleep through any discomfort)
3. Increase gradually (don't jump to 1000mg immediately)
4. Drink WATER (seriously) (5/11)

**6/11**
If stomach upset is severe:
- Tell your doctor BEFORE you stop
- Ask to try Extended-Release Metformin (gentler on the gut, costs a bit more)
- They may adjust your dose or timing
- There are alternatives if Metformin truly doesn't work for you

Never quit solo. (6/11)

**7/11**
Red flag: Diarrhea that lasts beyond 6 weeks, severe vomiting, or severe abdominal pain. These are NOT normal adjustment. Tell your doctor immediately. But mild diarrhea in week 1-2? That's your bacteria saying hello. (7/11)

**8/11**
Common question: "Is generic as good as brand (Glucophage)?"
YES. Same active ingredient. Same metabolism. Same results. The only difference is price and sometimes formulation (brand = smoother coating = maybe slightly easier on gut). But clinically? Identical. (8/11)

**9/11**
Kidney function check: Before you start Metformin, your doctor should test your kidney function (eGFR). If kidneys are weak, Metformin can accumulate and become unsafe. If your eGFR is below 30, your doctor will adjust or choose another drug. Annual checks after that. (9/11)

**10/11**
The 6-week rule: Give it 6 weeks with proper dosing before deciding it's not for you. Week 1 is rough. Week 3 is better. Week 6? Most people are fine. If you're still struggling at week 6, options exist. But don't judge too early. (10/11)

**11/11 - CTA**
AFYA helps track medication adherence (reminders, logs, side effects). You can share patterns with your doctor. Consistency is what works. Get the first 2 weeks right, and your body does the rest. Start tracking today. (11/11)

### BONUS THREAD: Gestational Diabetes in Nigeria (9 tweets)

**1/9 - HOOK**
You're pregnant. Doctor says you need a glucose tolerance test. You feel fine, so you skip it. Huge mistake. 1 in 7 Nigerian pregnancies has gestational diabetes. You don't feel it. Your baby does. This thread covers what you need to know. (1/9)

**2/9**
What is gestational diabetes?
High blood sugar during pregnancy. Your hormones and pregnancy weight stress your insulin. Normally your pancreas adapts. Sometimes it doesn't. Result: elevated glucose that can harm your baby and increase YOUR diabetes risk later. (2/9)

**3/9**
When and why it happens:
- Usually 2nd/3rd trimester
- 24–28 weeks is the standard testing window in Nigeria
- It goes away after delivery in most cases
- But 50% of women with gestational diabetes develop type 2 diabetes within 10 years

This matters long-term. (3/9)

**4/9**
Screening is a glucose tolerance test (GTT):
1. Drink a sugary liquid
2. Wait 1–2 hours
3. Blood test shows if you handled the glucose

Costs ₦5,000–10,000 at most private hospitals. Free/cheap at government facilities. Not optional. Do it. (4/9)

**5/9**
If you have gestational diabetes:
- You don't need medication initially (most cases managed with diet)
- Avoid white rice, sugary drinks, fried foods
- Eat more vegetables, lean protein, whole grains
- Walk 15–20 mins after meals
- Retest every 2 weeks until delivery

You can do this. (5/9)

**6/9**
What happens to your baby if untreated?
- Higher birth weight (causes delivery complications)
- Low blood sugar after birth (dangerous)
- Increased risk of childhood obesity and diabetes later
- Other metabolic complications

This is serious. Control matters. (6/9)

**7/9**
After pregnancy:
- Your glucose usually normalizes
- BUT get tested 6–12 weeks postpartum to confirm
- If it's still high, you have type 2 diabetes (not gestational anymore)
- Either way, annual testing for life (you're now high-risk)

Don't forget the follow-up. (7/9)

**8/9**
Prevention for next pregnancy:
- Lose weight if you were heavy after first pregnancy
- Exercise (30 mins daily walking counts)
- Avoid sugary drinks
- Eat fiber, protein, vegetables
- Get to a healthy BMI before next pregnancy

These changes lower risk dramatically. (8/9)

Type 2 Diabetes in Nigeria: What Every Adult Needs to Know

By AFYA Health Team  ·  March 2026  ·  6 min read

← Back to all articles

Nigeria is facing a quiet health crisis. The International Diabetes Federation estimates that over 11 million Nigerians are living with diabetes — and alarming research suggests that up to 60% of them have no idea. By the time many people receive a diagnosis, serious complications such as kidney disease, nerve damage, or vision loss may already be developing.

The good news: type 2 diabetes is largely preventable, and when caught early, it is manageable. Understanding your risk is the first and most important step.

What Is Type 2 Diabetes?

Type 2 diabetes occurs when your body either doesn't produce enough insulin or can't use it effectively. Insulin is the hormone that helps sugar (glucose) from food enter your cells for energy. When this process breaks down, sugar builds up in the bloodstream instead — damaging blood vessels and organs over time.

Unlike type 1 diabetes, which is an autoimmune condition, type 2 is strongly linked to lifestyle factors and develops gradually. Most people feel completely normal in the early stages, which is exactly why it goes undetected for so long.

Who Is Most at Risk in Nigeria?

Certain factors significantly raise your risk of developing type 2 diabetes. In the Nigerian context, these are worth paying close attention to:

  • Age over 40 — risk increases with age, though younger Nigerians are increasingly affected
  • Family history — having a parent or sibling with diabetes roughly doubles your risk
  • Overweight or obesity — excess weight, especially around the abdomen, is the strongest modifiable risk factor
  • Physical inactivity — sedentary work and low exercise levels are major contributors
  • High carbohydrate diet — diets heavy in white rice, white bread, and sugary drinks raise blood sugar consistently
  • High blood pressure — hypertension and diabetes frequently occur together
  • Gestational diabetes — women who had diabetes during pregnancy have a higher lifetime risk

💡 Did you know? Urban Nigerians face higher rates of type 2 diabetes than rural populations — largely due to dietary changes, reduced physical activity, and chronic work stress.

Warning Signs to Watch For

Because early-stage type 2 diabetes is often symptom-free, many people only notice something is wrong when the condition is more advanced. However, these signs may indicate elevated blood sugar and should prompt a visit to a healthcare provider:

  • Frequent urination, especially at night
  • Persistent thirst that doesn't go away after drinking
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Fatigue and low energy that doesn't improve with rest
  • Blurred or worsening vision
  • Slow-healing wounds or cuts
  • Tingling, numbness or pain in the feet or hands
  • Recurring skin, gum, or bladder infections

How Is It Diagnosed?

The only way to know for certain is a blood test. A fasting blood glucose test (done after not eating for at least 8 hours) or an HbA1c test (which reflects your average blood sugar over three months) can both confirm a diagnosis. Many private clinics and pharmacies across Lagos, Abuja, and other major cities offer these tests for between ₦2,000 and ₦5,000.

If you are over 35, overweight, or have any of the risk factors listed above, it is worth getting tested — even if you feel fine.

Managing Diabetes: What Actually Works

A diabetes diagnosis is not a life sentence. Millions of Nigerians are living well with the condition through a combination of:

  1. Dietary changes — reducing refined carbohydrates, eating more vegetables, legumes, and fibre-rich foods
  2. Regular physical activity — even 30 minutes of brisk walking five days a week measurably improves blood sugar control
  3. Medication adherence — taking prescribed medications consistently and never stopping without medical advice
  4. Regular monitoring — tracking blood glucose levels at home and attending check-up appointments
  5. Stress management — chronic stress raises blood sugar; sleep and mental health matter more than most people realise

The Bottom Line

Type 2 diabetes is one of the most common and most underdiagnosed conditions in Nigeria. The earlier it is caught, the easier it is to manage — and in some cases, lifestyle changes alone can reverse early-stage disease. If you have any risk factors, booking a blood glucose test is one of the most important things you can do for your long-term health this year.

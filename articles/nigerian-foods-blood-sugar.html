5 Nigerian Foods That Help Control Blood Sugar (And 3 to Watch)

By AFYA Health Team  ·  March 2026  ·  5 min read

← Back to all articles

One of the biggest fears people have after a diabetes diagnosis is that they must abandon the foods they love. Nigerian cuisine — with its rich stews, hearty soups, and carbohydrate-based staples — can seem incompatible with blood sugar management. The truth is more encouraging than that.

Many traditional Nigerian ingredients are nutritionally excellent for managing blood sugar. The key is not elimination, but understanding which foods work for you, which require moderation, and how preparation methods affect glycaemic impact.

📊 What is the glycaemic index? The glycaemic index (GI) measures how quickly a food raises blood sugar. Low GI foods (55 or below) cause a slower, more stable rise. High GI foods cause rapid spikes. For diabetes management, favouring lower GI options makes a significant difference.

5 Nigerian Foods That Support Blood Sugar Control

🫘
✅ Excellent choice

Beans (Brown, Black-eyed, Honey beans)

One of the most powerful foods for blood sugar management available to Nigerians. Beans are rich in protein, soluble fibre, and complex carbohydrates that digest slowly, causing a very gradual rise in blood sugar. A meal of ewa agoyin, moi moi, or akara provides sustained energy without dangerous spikes. The high protein content also promotes satiety, helping with portion control. Aim for beans 3–4 times per week.

🥬
✅ Excellent choice

Ugwu (Fluted Pumpkin Leaves)

Ugwu is a nutritional powerhouse used in soups across Nigeria. Research suggests that compounds in fluted pumpkin leaves may help improve insulin sensitivity and reduce blood glucose levels. It is also exceptionally high in vitamins A and C, iron, and calcium. Add ugwu generously to egusi, ofe onugbu, and other soups — it is virtually calorie-free and does not raise blood sugar.

🍆
✅ Excellent choice

Garden Eggs (African Eggplant)

Garden eggs are widely eaten raw or in sauces across Nigeria and are a genuinely useful food for diabetics. They have a very low glycaemic index, are high in fibre, and contain compounds that may inhibit enzymes linked to type 2 diabetes. Eating garden eggs with groundnut sauce as a snack is a traditional Nigerian habit that happens to be excellent for blood sugar management.

🐟
✅ Excellent choice

Fish (Tilapia, Catfish, Mackerel, Sardines)

Fish is a high-quality protein with zero carbohydrates, meaning it has no direct effect on blood sugar. It also provides omega-3 fatty acids that support heart health — important because people with diabetes have a significantly elevated risk of cardiovascular disease. Nigerian preparations like peppered fish, fish pepper soup, or fish added to vegetable soups are all excellent choices.

🥜
✅ Good choice

Groundnuts (Peanuts)

Groundnuts have a low glycaemic index and provide healthy fats, protein, and fibre that slow digestion and blunt blood sugar spikes. A small handful as a snack — or natural groundnut paste (without added sugar) eaten with vegetables — is a smart choice. Be mindful of portion size: groundnuts are calorie-dense, so a small fist-sized portion is enough.

3 Foods to Eat Carefully

These are not forbidden — context and portion size matter enormously. But these popular Nigerian foods can raise blood sugar quickly in large quantities:

🍚
⚠️ Moderate portions

White Rice

White rice is one of the highest-GI staples in the Nigerian diet. A large plate of white rice can cause a significant blood sugar spike. Practical approaches: halve your usual portion, mix with beans (which dramatically lowers the overall GI of the meal), switch to parboiled or basmati rice, and always pair with protein and vegetables.

🍞
⚠️ Choose carefully

White Bread and Agege Bread

Agege bread and similar soft white breads have a high glycaemic index and are often eaten in large quantities for breakfast. Switching to whole wheat or high-fibre bread slows the blood sugar impact considerably. If white bread is your only option, eat a smaller portion and always combine it with protein (eggs, sardines) and fat to blunt the spike.

🥤
⚠️ Significant risk

Sugary Drinks (Malt, Soft Drinks, Sweetened Zobo)

Liquid sugar is absorbed almost instantly into the bloodstream, causing sharp spikes in blood glucose. A bottle of Malta Guinness contains around 35g of sugar — nearly 9 teaspoons. For anyone managing diabetes or at risk of it, these drinks should be replaced with water, unsweetened zobo (hibiscus tea without sugar), or coconut water in modest quantities.

🍽️ Practical tip: The order in which you eat matters. Starting a meal with vegetables and protein before eating your carbohydrates can reduce the blood sugar spike by up to 30%, according to research. Eat your ugwu soup first, then your swallow — not the other way around.

The Bottom Line

Managing blood sugar with Nigerian food is entirely achievable. Build meals around beans, vegetables, fish, and lean meat. Reduce portions of refined carbohydrates and replace sugary drinks with water. Small, consistent changes in your daily diet make a measurable difference — and you do not have to give up the flavours that make Nigerian food wonderful.

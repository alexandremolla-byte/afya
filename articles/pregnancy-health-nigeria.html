Pregnancy in Nigeria: A Complete Health Guide for Expectant Mothers

By AFYA Health Team  ·  March 2026  ·  7 min read

← Back to all articles

Pregnancy is one of the most significant journeys a woman will ever take — and in Nigeria, that journey comes with unique challenges. Despite progress in maternal healthcare, Nigeria still accounts for a disproportionate share of global maternal deaths, with many of these being preventable through timely antenatal care, proper nutrition, and awareness of warning signs.

This guide is designed to give every expectant Nigerian mother — and her family — the practical knowledge needed to navigate pregnancy confidently, trimester by trimester.

Your First Trimester (Weeks 1–12)

The moment you receive a positive test, your health immediately becomes two people's health. The first trimester is a critical period of rapid foetal development — your baby's brain, heart, spine, and limbs are forming in these early weeks.

Start folic acid immediately. A daily supplement of 400–800 micrograms of folic acid dramatically reduces the risk of neural tube defects such as spina bifida. This is most effective in the first four weeks — which is often before women even know they are pregnant. If you are planning to conceive, start folic acid now.

Book your first antenatal visit. Ideally within the first 10 weeks. Your healthcare provider will confirm the pregnancy, estimate your due date, screen for anaemia and infections, and set the foundation for monitoring the rest of your pregnancy.

Common first-trimester experiences include nausea, fatigue, breast tenderness, and frequent urination. These are usually normal. However, severe vomiting that prevents you from keeping any food or fluid down (hyperemesis gravidarum) requires medical attention.

Your Second Trimester (Weeks 13–26)

For many women, the second trimester brings welcome relief — nausea fades, energy returns, and the pregnancy becomes visible. This is also the period for an important anatomy scan (typically around 18–22 weeks), which checks your baby's development and confirms the position of the placenta.

Iron deficiency anaemia is one of the most common complications of pregnancy in Nigeria, affecting up to 60% of pregnant women. You may be prescribed iron tablets — take them consistently, ideally with a source of vitamin C (like orange juice) to improve absorption, and avoid taking them with tea or milk which can reduce uptake.

Monitor your blood pressure at every visit. Rising blood pressure during pregnancy can be an early sign of preeclampsia, a serious condition that can be life-threatening if not managed. Know your numbers.

Your Third Trimester (Weeks 27–40)

The final stretch is about preparation — physically, practically, and emotionally. Antenatal visits should now be every two to four weeks, then weekly as your due date approaches.

This is when you should finalise your birth plan and ensure you know exactly where you will deliver, how you will get there quickly if labour begins unexpectedly, and who will support you. In Nigeria, delays in reaching facility care are a leading contributor to adverse birth outcomes — having a plan in place is not optional.

Nutrition During Pregnancy in Nigeria

The good news: Nigerian cuisine can be highly nutritious during pregnancy. Focus on including:

  • Protein — beans, lentils, eggs, fish, chicken, and beef (well-cooked)
  • Leafy greens — ugwu (fluted pumpkin), spinach, bitter leaf — rich in iron and folate
  • Complex carbohydrates — yam, plantain, sweet potato over refined white rice and bread
  • Calcium — dairy products, crayfish, and small fish with edible bones
  • Hydration — at least 8–10 glasses of clean water daily

Avoid unpasteurised dairy, raw or undercooked meat and fish, and excessive caffeine. Limit sugar-sweetened drinks, which can contribute to gestational diabetes.

🚨 Go to hospital immediately if you experience: severe headache + blurred vision, sudden swelling of face/hands/feet, reduced baby movement for more than 12 hours, heavy bleeding, or severe abdominal pain. These can be signs of preeclampsia or other emergencies.

Antenatal Visits: How Many Do You Need?

The World Health Organisation recommends a minimum of 8 antenatal care contacts during pregnancy. In Nigeria, many women attend fewer than four — often due to cost, distance, or underestimating their importance. Each visit serves a specific purpose: screening for complications, updating vaccinations, monitoring foetal growth, and preparing for birth.

If cost is a barrier, government primary healthcare centres offer free or heavily subsidised antenatal care. Do not skip visits to save money — the cost of complications is always far higher.

After Birth: The Fourth Trimester

The first six weeks after delivery are as critical as the pregnancy itself. Your body is recovering from a profound physical event, and postnatal depression — though rarely discussed in Nigeria — affects a significant proportion of new mothers. If you feel persistently sad, anxious, or disconnected from your baby after giving birth, please speak to a healthcare provider. This is a medical condition, not a moral failing.

Attend all scheduled postnatal visits, continue iron supplementation if prescribed, and if breastfeeding, ensure you are eating and drinking adequately to support milk production.

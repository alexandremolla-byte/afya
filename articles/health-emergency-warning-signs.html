10 Health Warning Signs You Should Never Ignore in Nigeria

By AFYA Health Team  ·  March 2026  ·  4 min read

← Back to all articles

In Nigeria, a combination of limited healthcare access, financial pressure, and cultural norms around "managing" discomfort at home means that many serious health conditions go untreated until they become emergencies. Stroke, heart attack, and severe infections all have far better outcomes when treated quickly.

This list is not meant to cause alarm. Most headaches are just headaches, and most chest discomfort passes without incident. But these specific symptoms, in specific circumstances, are your body's emergency signals. Recognising them — and acting on them immediately — can be the difference between recovery and permanent harm.

🚨 For all items on this list: Do not wait until morning. Do not try herbal remedies first. Go to a hospital emergency room or call for emergency transport immediately.

1

Sudden, severe headache — "the worst of your life"

A headache that comes on suddenly and is described as the worst pain ever felt in the head is a classic warning sign of a subarachnoid haemorrhage — bleeding in the brain. This is a life-threatening emergency. Unlike a tension headache that builds gradually, this one is explosive in onset. Go to hospital immediately.

2

Face drooping, arm weakness, or slurred speech

These are the hallmark signs of a stroke. Use the FAST test: Face (is one side drooping?), Arms (can they raise both equally?), Speech (is it slurred or strange?), Time (call for help immediately). Every minute of a stroke causes the death of approximately 2 million brain cells. Speed of treatment determines outcome. Do not drive the person yourself if possible — call for help so treatment can begin en route.

3

Chest pain, pressure, or tightness

Chest pain that is described as pressure, squeezing, or heaviness — especially spreading to the arm, jaw, or back — is the classic presentation of a heart attack. However, Nigerian patients often present atypically: nausea, extreme fatigue, or abdominal pain may be the primary symptom, particularly in women. Any unexplained chest discomfort lasting more than a few minutes requires emergency evaluation.

4

Difficulty breathing at rest

Sudden shortness of breath without exertion — particularly if accompanied by chest pain, rapid heart rate, or coughing up blood — can indicate a pulmonary embolism (blood clot in the lungs), severe asthma attack, or acute heart failure. All of these are medical emergencies requiring immediate care.

5

Loss of consciousness or unresponsiveness

Any episode of complete unresponsiveness — even briefly — must be evaluated by a doctor. In a Nigerian context, this could be a seizure (including first-time seizures in adults, which require investigation), diabetic hypoglycaemia, cardiac arrhythmia, or a neurological event. Place the person in the recovery position, clear the airway, and seek emergency care.

6

High fever with severe headache and stiff neck

This combination is the cardinal sign of meningitis — infection of the membranes surrounding the brain. In Nigeria, meningococcal meningitis outbreaks occur periodically, and bacterial meningitis can kill within hours if untreated. Sensitivity to light (photophobia) and a non-blanching rash are additional warning signs. This requires emergency hospital care and IV antibiotics.

7

Sudden vision changes or loss

Abrupt loss of vision in one or both eyes, sudden appearance of floaters or flashes of light, or a curtain-like shadow across the visual field can indicate retinal detachment, stroke affecting the visual cortex, or acute glaucoma. These are time-sensitive conditions — delay in treatment can result in permanent blindness. Go directly to an eye clinic or hospital emergency department.

8

Coughing or vomiting blood

Coughing up blood (haemoptysis) can indicate tuberculosis — still prevalent in Nigeria — pulmonary embolism, or lung cancer. Vomiting blood or passing black, tarry stools suggests a bleed in the upper gastrointestinal tract, often from a peptic ulcer. Both require urgent investigation. TB in particular is highly treatable but must be diagnosed early.

9

Severe abdominal pain that is constant and worsening

Pain that is severe, constant, and progressively worsening — especially when the abdomen becomes rigid or tender to touch — can indicate appendicitis, intestinal obstruction, ectopic pregnancy (in women of reproductive age), or peritonitis. These conditions can be fatal if surgery is delayed. Seek emergency care; do not take painkillers and wait at home.

10

Thoughts of harming yourself or others

Mental health emergencies are medical emergencies. If you or someone you know is experiencing thoughts of suicide, self-harm, or violence, seek help immediately. In Nigeria, the Mentally Aware Nigeria Initiative (MANI) provides mental health support, and many teaching hospitals have psychiatry departments that can provide urgent assessment. You are not alone, and effective help is available.

A Note on Accessing Emergency Care in Nigeria

Emergency care infrastructure in Nigeria varies significantly. In Lagos and Abuja, dedicated emergency departments exist at major teaching hospitals and a growing number of private hospitals. Federal Medical Centres across the country also provide emergency services. Where ambulance services are limited, coordinating with a private driver, okada, or keke napep can be life-saving — what matters most is getting to a facility quickly.

Know your nearest emergency facility before you need it. Save the number. Tell your family.
